## 0. Principle

Copy is the fastest way to look junior or senior. For this audience — GCC government and enterprise C-suite — **restraint reads as seniority; hype reads as a freelancer.** Every line obeys one rule above all (PRD §4): *if a claim wouldn't survive a reference check, it doesn't ship.* This guide turns Brand §2 voice into mechanics anyone (or Claude Code) can apply consistently.

Words are design material, not decoration (per frontend-design principles): they exist to help the buyer understand and act, never to impress.

---

## 1. Voice in One Paragraph

V47 writes like a senior advisor talking to a peer across a table: precise, candid, systems-minded, and understated. It leads with the buyer's problem, not V47's greatness. It shows (a demo, a number, a named method) rather than asserts. It says plainly what's delivered versus in progress. It never sells with adjectives — the facts do the selling.

---

## 2. The Rules (mechanics)

1. **Lead with the problem, then the answer.** The buyer cares about their stalled AI pilot, not your mission.
2. **Active voice, present tense.** "We take you from strategy to production," not "Clients are taken on a journey."
3. **Sentence case everywhere** (headings, buttons). Not Title Case. ALL CAPS only for mono eyebrows.
4. **Specific beats clever.** "Prototype in 3 weeks" beats "We move fast."
5. **One idea per sentence.** Short sentences read senior. Cut every clause that isn't earning its place.
6. **Verbs name the exact action.** A button that says "Book a session" leads to a page about booking a session; the confirmation says "Session booked."
7. **Numbers are tabular and true.** Every metric sourced; no rounding up for effect.
8. **Honest status in words, not just badges.** "Partnering with X" not "delivered for X"; "in discussion" not "trusted by."
9. **No adjective stacks.** One precise noun beats three vague adjectives.
10. **Cut, then cut again.** Chanel's mirror rule applies to copy: remove one word before you publish.

---

## 3. Banned Vocabulary (enforce at edit time)

*leading · cutting-edge · revolutionary · world-class · game-changing · next-gen · seamless · synergy · unlock / unleash · empower · robust (as filler) · passionate · ninja / rockstar / guru · "leverage" (as filler) · "solutions" (as a catch-all noun) · "in today's fast-paced world" · "we are excited to…"*

Use **"transform"** at most once per page. No emoji in body/headings (LinkedIn captions may use them sparingly). No exclamation marks in body copy.

---

## 4. Before → After (the rewrite discipline)

| ❌ Generic / inflated | ✅ V47 |
|---|---|
| "We are a leading provider of end-to-end AI solutions." | "We take governments and enterprises from AI strategy to production." |
| "Trusted by top government leaders across the region." | "Five years building a national government BI platform used by 120,000+ people across 14 ministries." |
| "Our cutting-edge AI unlocks transformative value." | "Most AI pilots stall before production. We're built to get them there." |
| "We are passionate about innovation." | "We build working prototypes you can click, not slideware." |
| "Contact us to learn how we can help your business." | "Book a discovery session." |
| "Head of AI at [Client]." *(if untrue)* | "Partnering with [Client] on their AI roadmap." |

---

## 5. Headline Patterns (with restraint)

- **Positioning (hero):** *"Building AI-Native Enterprises."* (declarative, no verb inflation)
- **Problem-first (sections/articles):** *"Why AI pilots stall before production."*
- **Capability (proof):** *"A national BI platform. 120,000+ users. 14 ministries."*
- **Method (frameworks):** *"How we take AI from idea to production."*
- **Avoid:** questions as headlines ("Ready to transform your business?"), hype triplets, and "The future of X."

---

## 6. CTA Library (sentence case, active verb)

| Context | Primary | Secondary |
|---|---|---|
| Hero | Book an AI discovery session | Explore our work |
| Case study | Book a session | See how we work → (Methods) |
| Framework | — (soft) | Talk to us about this → |
| Lab entry | Book a session | Read the architecture ↓ |
| Contact band | Book a session | Email · LinkedIn |

Rules: no "Submit," no "Learn more" without an object, no urgency manipulation ("Don't miss out"). The label describes exactly what happens next.

---

## 7. Eyebrows & Labels (mono, uppercase, tracked)

Short, factual, category-setting: `BOUTIQUE AI CONSULTING · ABU DHABI` · `CASE STUDY` · `AI LAB` · `FRAMEWORK` · `GOVTECH`. Never a sentence; never hype.

---

## 8. Microcopy Standards (frontend-design §"writing")

- **Empty states** = direction, not mood: "No case studies in this sector yet — see all work →." Never a sad-face or apology.
- **Errors** = what happened + how to fix, in the interface's voice: "That email address looks incomplete. Check and try again." Errors don't apologise and aren't vague.
- **Confirmations** = mirror the action verb: `Book a session` → toast "Session booked."
- **Loading** = quiet and factual; no "AI is thinking…" theatrics.
- **Alt text** = describe the content's function, not "image of."

## 10. Case Study Copy Discipline (T3)

- Lead with the **result** as the eyebrow metric.
- Write each of the 8 sections (PRD §9) factually; resist adjectives — let the numbers and architecture carry it.
- Confidentiality: "a MENA bank," "a national ministry programme" — never inflate to compensate for anonymity.
- "Key Learnings" is candid, including trade-offs. Candour reads as senior.

---

## 11. The One-Line Test

Before publishing any sentence, ask:
1. Would it survive a reference check? (If no → rewrite or cut.)
2. Could a generic AI agency have written it? (If yes → make it specific to V47.)
3. Is there a shorter, plainer version? (If yes → use it.)

Pass all three, or it doesn't ship.
